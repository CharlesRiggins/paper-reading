# LoRA Without Regret

arXiv: Blog Post, DOI: 10.64434/tml.20250929 | John Schulman and Thinking Machines Lab (Thinking Machines Lab, 2025)
Code: —

## Files

- `s01_overview.md` — Introduces the practical motivation for parameter-efficient fine-tuning (**PEFT**) in post-training: base models may contain trillions of parameters, while post-training datasets are often much smaller and narrower. It defines LoRA as replacing each weight matrix $W$ with $W' = W + \gamma BA$, then explains operational advantages such as multi-tenant serving, smaller training layouts, and fast adapter transfer. The section frames the central question of the blog: whether LoRA can match full fine-tuning (**FullFT**) in sample efficiency and ultimate performance, and previews the “low-regret regime” where it does.

- `s02_methods_rank_batch.md` — Covers the experimental setup and the first supervised fine-tuning results. The authors sweep LoRA ranks from 1 to 512 and learning rates across `Llama 3` and `Qwen3` models on `Tulu3` and `OpenThoughts3`, finding that high-rank LoRA and FullFT follow nearly identical loss curves while low-rank LoRA slows once adapter capacity is exhausted. It also highlights a consistent learning-rate pattern: optimal LoRA LR is about **10x** FullFT. A separate batch-size experiment shows LoRA can be less tolerant of large batches, producing a widening loss gap that appears tied to the product-of-matrices parametrization rather than rank.

- `s03_layers_rl.md` — Explains where LoRA should be applied and why attention-only LoRA underperforms. Experiments on dense `Llama-3.1-8B` and sparse `Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base` show that applying LoRA to MLP/MoE layers is crucial; attention-only rank 256 can underperform MLP-only rank 128 despite similar parameter counts. The section then turns to reinforcement learning, where LoRA matches FullFT even at rank 1 on math reasoning tasks. The authors argue this is expected because policy-gradient RL conveys roughly $O(1)$ bits per episode, far less information than supervised token-level learning.

- `s04_hyperparameters.md` — Details the LoRA hyperparameter analysis around $W' = W + \frac{\alpha}{r}BA$. The $1/r$ scaling explains why early learning curves and short-run optimal learning rates are approximately rank-independent, because $(1/r)\sum_i \Delta_i$ behaves like an average of rank-1 updates with equal expectation. The section derives invariances among $\alpha$, $\text{init}_A$, $LR_A$, and $LR_B$, reducing four apparent knobs to a two-dimensional effective space. It also reports a 14-model learning-rate sweep where the fitted LoRA multiplier over FullFT is **9.8**, with short runs potentially preferring about **15x**.

- `s05_discussion.md` — Synthesizes the two conditions under which LoRA behaves like FullFT: LoRA should be applied to all major parameter-containing layers, and the adapter should not be capacity-constrained relative to the information in the dataset. The authors connect the layer result to empirical neural tangent kernel reasoning, where LoRA approximates FullFT only if it covers the layers dominating gradient dot products. They discuss capacity estimates for supervised learning and RL, derive why LoRA requires slightly more than $\frac{2}{3}$ of FullFT forward-backward FLOPs per weight matrix, and list open questions about forecasting LoRA performance, explaining LR ratios, evaluating variants like `PiSSA`, and handling MoE adapters.

- `s06_closing_citation.md` — Contains the blog’s closing motivation, acknowledgements, and citation information. Thinking Machines Lab presents LoRA as a way to make fine-tuning more accessible and customizable, while also motivating deeper research into model capacity, dataset complexity, and sample efficiency. The file preserves the requested plain-text citation and BibTeX entry for John Schulman and Thinking Machines Lab’s 2025 blog post.
